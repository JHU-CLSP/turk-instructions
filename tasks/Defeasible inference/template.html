For this task, given a premise and a hypothesis you will:

  • Write a weakener in one sentence.
  • Write a strengthener in one sentence.

Remember:

  • The premise sentence describes a real-world situation and is always assumed to be true.
  • The hypothesis sentence describes an assumption or inference that we might make about that situation having read the premise. In most cases, the hypothesis statement is very likely to be true given the premise; however, it is not necessarily guaranteed to be true.
  • A weakener is a statement that weakens the hypothesis;
    it makes us much less likely to believe the hypothesis is true.
  • A strengthener is a statement that strengthens the hypothesis;
    it makes us much more likely to believe the hypothesis is true.

Rules:

  • DO write in complete sentences.
  • DO use the "impossible" checkbox when appropriate! The use may be rare, but we do expect some.
  • For weakeners:
    • DO NOT contradict the premise
    • DO NOT simply negate the hypothesis
    • DO NOT directly contradict the hypothesis
  • For strengtheners:
    • DO NOT contradict the premise
    • DO NOT repeat or rephrase the contents of the hypothesis
EXPLANATION

You will be presented with two sentences, called a premise and a hypothesis, respectively. (See example below.)

  • The premise sentence describes a real-world situation and is always assumed to be true.
  • The hypothesis sentence describes an assumption or inference that we might make about that situation having read the premise.
  • In most cases, the hypothesis statement is very likely to be true given the premise; however, it is not necessarily guaranteed to be true.

You will provide additional information about the situation that might weaken or strengthen the hypothesis.

  • A weakener is a statement that weakens the hypothesis;
    it makes us much less likely to believe the hypothesis is true.
    • ⇨ TIP: Start by thinking of a situation where the premise is true but the hypothesis is wrong.
  • A strengthener is a statement that strengthens the hypothesis;
    it makes us much more likely to believe the hypothesis is true.
    • ⇨ TIP: Start by thinking of a situation where both the premise and the hypothesis are true.

IMPORTANT! Please note the order: weakener will always come first, followed by strengthener.

EXAMPLE
premise “A female swimmer in a blue cap is swimming underwater.”
hypothesis “The woman is a competitive swimmer.”
  Response Explanation of why
the response is good/bad
GOOD
weakener		 “She is swimming in a hotel resort pool.” If we learn the swimmer is in a hotel resort pool, that is less likely that the woman is a competitive swimmer.
BAD
weakeners		 “The woman is not a competitive swimmer." This is a direct negation of the hypothesis.
“The woman is a recreational swimmer.” Rather than using additional information or evidence to weaken, this weakener directly contradicts the hypothesis by changing a descriptive word.
“The woman is standing in the shallow end of the pool.” This directly contradicts the premise that states that the woman is swimming.
GOOD
strengthener		 “There is a large crowd that is watching her swim.” For example, if we learn that there is a large crowd watching the woman swimming, that would strengthen the inference she is a competitive swimmer.
BAD
strengtheners		 “People agree that the woman is a competitive swimmer." This does not provide additional information and repeats the content of the hypothesis.
“The woman is really a competitive swimmer.” This simply repeats the hypothesis using the word of emphasis like really.

Example 1:

premise: A girl in a black t-shirt laughs at a man in a black t-shirt who is telling a story.
hypothesis: The man tells a funny story.
weakener: The girl is laughing to be polite.
strengthener: The man is making a goofy face and gesturing wildly.

Example 2:

premise: A woman is looking at a photo of herself with two kids.
hypothesis: She is a mother of two children.
weakener: The woman is a school teacher.
strengthener: There is a mess of toys all over her living room.

Example 3:

premise: There is a woman who is taking a photo of a flower.
hypothesis: She is outdoors.
weakener: The woman works at a greenhouse.
strengthener: Set behind the flower is the bright blue sky.

Example 4:

premise: A first girl is holding a toy and a second girl is on the ground crying.
hypothesis: The first girl is a mean kid.
weakener: The second girl has a scrape on her knee.
strengthener: The toy belongs to the second girl.

The sentences you write here will be used in experiments to teach Artificial Intelligence (AI) systems how to make nuanced inferences about real-world situations usually involving people. It is important that these AI systems treat all people fairly, regardless of their race, ethnicity, religion, gender, sexuality, ability, or other aspects of personal identity. Therefore, when you perform this task, please bear this in mind.

Please AVOID writing sentences that involve making stereotyped or
prejudicial assumptions about people based on their identities.

Example:

premise: A person is typing at a computer.
hypothesis: The person is writing code.

  • Stereotyped or prejudicial responses to avoid:
    • weakener: "The person is wearing lipstick and a dress."
    strengthener: "The person is an overweight man drinking Mountain Dew."
    Explanation: These sentences rely on gender and identity stereotypes about computer programmers.
  • Acceptable identity neutral responses:
    • weakener: "They are standing behind a hotel reception desk."
    strengthener: "Next to the person lies an open book on JavaScript programming."

If you absolutely cannot think of a strengthener or weakener that does not violate this guideline, then please select the "impossible" checkbox, followed by the appropriate selection. This option should be used very sparingly.
